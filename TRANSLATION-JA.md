# Japanese (ja) Translation Guide — The Gender Dysphoria Bible

This is the shared style/terminology guide for the Japanese localization of
**The Gender Dysphoria Bible** (genderdysphoria.fyi). Every translator follows it
so that 22 chapters read as one consistent, warm, accurate book.

Japanese title of the work: **「性別違和バイブル」** (subtitle / brand may keep "The Gender Dysphoria Bible").

---

## 1. Voice & register (tone)

- **Body prose: です・ます調.** Warm, supportive, plain-spoken — the voice of a kind, well-informed friend, not a textbook. The English speaks directly to "you"; in Japanese, usually drop the subject, and use 「あなた」 only when it reads naturally. Never lecture.
- **Dictionary-style definition blockquotes (the 「Gen·der — *noun*: …」 boxes): である調 (plain form).** This matches the source's dictionary tone and the Korean edition. Example: 「…を指す。」
- Preserve the source's **validation and reassurance**. When English says things like "your identity is valid no matter how it manifests," render it with full warmth: 「その現れ方がどうであれ、あなたのアイデンティティは正当なものです。」 Do not soften, hedge, or clinicalize the supportive passages.
- Keep humor / casual asides casual. The book occasionally jokes; keep it light, don't formalize it away.
- Gender-neutral where the English is neutral. Don't introduce gendered assumptions the source didn't make.
- Avoid stiff translationese. Reorder clauses, split long sentences, and use natural Japanese connectives. Aim for prose a Japanese reader would never guess was translated.

### Diagnostic manuals (be careful)
- **DSM-5** → DSM-5（『精神疾患の診断・統計マニュアル 第5版』）. Its diagnosis "Gender Dysphoria" → 性別違和.
- **ICD-11** → ICD-11（『国際疾病分類 第11版』）. Its diagnosis "Gender Incongruence" → 性別不合.
- **Gender Identity Disorder (GID)** → 性同一性障害（GID、旧称）. This is the **obsolete** diagnosis; the book critiques it. Use ONLY when referring to the historical/outdated term, **never** as a label for trans people today.

## 3. Formatting rules — DO NOT BREAK THESE

The pages are Markdown extended with Handlebars. Mishandling the code breaks the build.

1. **Preserve every `{!{ … }!}` block and every `{{ … }}` expression exactly.** These are code, not prose. Inside them, translate ONLY human-readable strings:
   - `caption="…"`, `alt="…"`, and visible HTML text (inside `<blockquote>`, `<div>`, `<span>`, `<em>`, `<strong>`, etc.).
   - Do **NOT** translate: helper names (`import`, `img`, `tweet`, `icon`), variable references (`images.titlecard`, `meta.tweets`, `page.lang`), attribute **keys**, `className`/`class` values, `srcSizes`, `style` values, `link=` URLs, `external=1`, IDs.
2. **Image references stay identical**: `images.titlecard`, `images.bimodal`, etc. Never rename.
3. **URLs stay as-is** (keep the English Wikipedia / external links). Do not swap to ja.wikipedia.
4. **Markdown structure is preserved**: same heading levels (`#`/`##`/`###`), lists, numbering, blockquotes, bold/italic, footnotes, tables, horizontal rules, and the `<hr class="print-break-after …">` HTML.
5. **Headings**: translate the heading text (per glossary). Keep the level.
6. **Tweets**: keep `{{import '~/tweet' ids=[ … ] tweets=meta.tweets … }}` blocks and the frontmatter `tweets:` IDs byte-for-byte. Tweet body text is injected by the build; don't touch it.
7. **Do not add or remove sections, paragraphs, images, or tweets.** Match the source's structure 1:1. (You may split/merge sentences within a paragraph for natural Japanese.)

## 4. Frontmatter rules

Keep all keys. Translate only the human-readable VALUES.

- `date`: **unchanged** (copy from the English file).
- `title`: translate (Japanese chapter title — see per-chapter assignment).
- `description`: translate (this is the social/SEO description; make it a natural one-sentence JP summary).
- `linkTitle`: translate if present.
- `preBody: '_disclaimer'`: keep exactly.
- `classes`: keep exactly (e.g. `- gdb`).
- `tweets:`: keep the same list of IDs exactly (if present).
- `lang: ja`: **add this** (every chapter).
- `siblings:`: set `prev`/`next` to the `/ja/<slug>` URLs given in your per-chapter assignment, and translate `prevCaption`/`nextCaption` to the given Japanese captions.

Each translator receives an exact per-chapter assignment (filename, title, description hint, prev/next links + captions). Use it verbatim for slugs and navigation so cross-links stay consistent.

## 5. Quality bar

This should read as a **great introduction for Japanese readers** — fluent, warm, accurate, and welcoming to someone questioning their gender. When in doubt, prioritize (1) emotional fidelity to the source's supportive voice, (2) terminological consistency with this guide, (3) natural Japanese, in that order. Consult the Korean (`public/ko/`), Chinese (`public/zh/`, `public/tw/`), and German (`public/de/`) editions for how tricky passages were handled, but the **English (`public/en/`) is the authoritative source**.

## 6. Translation philosophy — domesticate the voice, foreignize the world

The GDB is two things at once: a **lifeline** (a questioning reader must feel *seen and validated* — the worst failure is a cold sentence, not an awkward one) and a **medical reference** (dosages, biology, and diagnostic criteria must be exact). Optimize for both, in that priority order when they conflict.

The guiding principle:

- **Domesticate everything the reader feels with.** Register (です・ます warmth + である in the dictionary boxes), terminology (this glossary; community-current; never `性同一性障害` as a synonym), rhythm and flow should make a Japanese reader's *heart* feel at home — the prose must not read as translated.
- **Foreignize the author's lived world.** Her personal anecdotes and US/Anglo cultural references (bar/bat mitzvah, prom, NFL, Sears catalog, pickle jars, the *Clue* app, GLAAD…) are *real testimony* — the book's authority comes from authentic personal narrative. **Keep them; add a brief gloss only where comprehension actually breaks.** Do not replace them with Japanese equivalents — that fabricates a memory the author never had.
- **Keep the punch.** The source has deliberate rhetoric — ALL-CAPS warnings (**EXTREMELY RISKY**), cheeky sign-offs ("That's dysphoria, baby."), wry asides. Preserve *register and rhetorical beat*, not just propositional meaning; the personality is part of why the book reaches people. Don't flatten it into neutral exposition.
- **One voice, end to end.** The whole book must read as one warm, consistent narrator. Keep terminology and tone uniform across all 22 chapters; avoid a patchwork of different drafting styles.
- **Accuracy is sacred in the medical chapters.** No drift on numbers/biology/diagnoses, even when the source coins a nonstandard term (render faithfully and, if useful, note it).

In one line: *make the reader feel the book was written for their heart, in their language and their community's words — while keeping it honestly the author's own American, lived, witty testimony.*

## 7. English glosses & acronyms (house style)

- **English word/name glosses use superscript** `<sup>(english)</sup>` placed right after the Japanese/katakana term — matching the Korean edition and the `ジェンダーとは` chapter. Use for: transliterated English terms (`トランスセクシュアル<sup>(transsexual)</sup>`), technical terms (`遺伝子型<sup>(genotype)</sup>`), coinages (`<sup>(catch-22)</sup>`), and proper names (`<sup>(John Money)</sup>`). Gloss **once**, at first/term-defining mention.
- **Acronyms stay inline in full-width parens**: `（AFAB）`, `（HRT）`, `（DSM-5）`, `（PCOS）`, `（WPATH）`, `（SOC）` — *not* superscript (they get reused standalone later). Normalize casing to the source's canonical form (e.g. **SOC**, **DSM-5**).
- **Japanese-content parentheticals stay inline**: `（高揚感）`, `（味方）`, `（トランジション）`.
- When the text **discusses a term as a term** (etymology, coinage, definition), always show the English — every non-Latin-script sibling edition (Korean, Chinese, Greek) does. Latin-script editions don't, because their cognates are already recognizable; Japanese, like Korean, needs the gloss.
